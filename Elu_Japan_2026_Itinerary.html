A note on this itinerary

This is the real version — the one where a typhoon cancelled Okinawa two days before departure and we rebooked an entire leg of the trip in an afternoon. What follows is what actually happened, including every detour, every line worth waiting in, and every door at the end of an alley worth walking through.

The typhoon did us a favor.

Our Okinawa diving trip was cancelled two days before departure. We pivoted to the opposite side of Japan — Sapporo, Hokkaido. Agoda cancelled our hotel with no fees. Trip.com charged $10 for the flight change. United miles cancelled penalty-free. We rebooked on ANA using United miles and were sorted within hours. Always book refundable. Always travel with points. Always leave room for the trip to surprise you.

Tokyo

Kantō · Days 1–4

Tokyo is best understood through its neighborhoods — each one a different frequency. My favorites: Ebisu for a night out, Meguro City for calm shopping and coffee, and Shimokitazawa for vintage, elevated coffee, and a homie feel. We stayed in Roppongi — beautiful, posher.

On queuing in Japan
Lines of one hour or more are completely normal at exceptional small restaurants in Japan. It is not a deterrent — it is a signal. The locals know. Charge your phone, bring water, and do not let the anxiety win. The bowl at the end makes it make sense.

Kyoto

Kansai · Days 5–6

The city that asks you to slow down. Get lost in its small streets. Enjoy beautiful matcha and coffee shops, standing bars, and incredible food.

Getting there
Tokyo → Kyoto on the Nozomi Shinkansen (bullet train). Download the app and book online. Get a bento box for the ride. We bought a JR West 5-day pass online — covers all trains to Uji, Okayama, and Hiroshima. A great find.

Okayama

Chūgoku · Days 7–9

A castle city with one of the country's great gardens, and the gateway to Naoshima. Slower than Tokyo, more personal than Kyoto.

Getting there
Included in JR West pass — take the Shinkansen. Cars 1 and 2 are usually for unreserved seats. To reserve, use the green ticketing machines at any station.
What to eat in Okayama
Okayama Barazushi (colorful scattered seafood sushi), Demi-Katsudon (pork cutlet with demi-glace), fresh fruit parfaits, Kibi-dango dumplings, Hiruzen Yakisoba (miso noodles), and Seto Inland Sea seafood — particularly Spanish mackerel.

Naoshima

Seto Inland Sea · Day 8

One of the best days of my life. A small island that decided art was the answer — explore it by foot, bus, or my preferred way: e-bike. World-class museums. No cars.

Getting there · Bring cash
From Okayama Station on the JR Uno Line → Uno Station → walk to ferry terminal → ferry to Miyanoura Port. Across from the port, rent e-bikes (cash only) or take the Town Bus or Benesse Shuttle toward the Chichu area.
Élu on Naoshima
Rent bikes. Walk through the Chichu Garden before entering the museum — the transition from nature to concrete is part of the experience. The Walter De Maria and James Turrell rooms rely entirely on the angle of morning light. Go early. Everything on this island rewards going early. My favorite museum in the world.
Book ahead
Chichu Art Museum
Tadao Ando's subterranean masterpiece. Permanent works by Monet, Walter De Maria, and James Turrell. The building is as much the art as the art. Book timed entry tickets in advance.

Sapporo

Hokkaido · Days 10–11

The typhoon plan. We had never researched Sapporo. We had no expectations. That turned out to be exactly right.

The unplanned chapter.

When the typhoon cancelled Okinawa, we rebooked everything in an afternoon — ANA flights with United miles, a stunning Onsen Ryokan with immediate availability. Sapporo gave us seafood, curry, whisky, a beer museum, and a hidden bar in an alley where the owner pulled out the karaoke and became the Japanese version of someone's mom. The typhoon did us a favor.

Departure · June 7

Last Meal Before Flying

The last meal · Narita Airport Terminal 1
Dashi Chazuke. I went to this place three years ago and was still thinking about it. How is it that you can get this amazing meal at an airport? Only in Japan. Drop your bags, and before passing security go get yourself a dashi chazuke — warm, comforting food before a long flight. Service is fast, no need to worry about time.
